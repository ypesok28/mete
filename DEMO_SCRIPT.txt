PHASE 1 — THE HOOK & THE PROBLEM  (about 0:10-0:50)

Picture one operator at a forward edge node. One 3D printer. A finite pile of
carbon-fiber filament, a few printer-hours, and a tank of generator fuel —
already rationed, because resupply is days out. And a mission to cover tonight,
before the window closes.

The decision that owns their night isn't which design to pull off a shelf. It's
what do I build from this pile — and how many? Spend a gram, an hour, a watt on
the wrong airframe, and it's gone before the mission even starts.

Right now, that call is a whiteboard guess. And in an era drowning in AI hype,
here's the part everyone misses: you cannot hand this decision to a model that
guesses. A hallucination — or one quiet math error — at the tactical edge
doesn't waste a spool of filament. It loses the mission, and it can cost lives.
The edge doesn't need an AI oracle. It needs absolute, deterministic truth.


PHASE 2 — THE PRODUCT & THE STAGE WOW  (about 0:50-1:45)

Mete is a fully offline, deterministic decision engine, powered by Google's
CP-SAT solver. It takes the hard physical constraints and the live mission
profile, and returns the mathematically optimal build plan — in under a second,
on this laptop, with the network off.

Let me show you. (point to screen) Right now, for a wide-area reconnaissance
mission under tight fuel and feedstock, Mete says: build five small Scout
drones — and it can't reach a sixth, because the filament binds.

Watch what happens when I change the reality on the ground. (swap the mission
to DEEP STRIKE: one hardened target, 120 km out) Now the mission is a single
hardened, high-value target, a hundred and twenty kilometers out. (it re-solves
live) The five Scouts dissolve — and one heavy Strike-L snaps into place. The
binding constraint flips to Range. Same printer, same two kilos of filament —
but the math just reallocated every gram to fit the mission, before we burned

And this is not a canned animation. (reframe the target to a light sensor —

number — one kilometer, a hundred and fifty. (judge types; it solves live)
Closer in, it picks the cheapest airframe that works; farther out, it steps up
a tier; past what the catalog can reach, it tells you so. You typed that — I
didn't. It's a deterministic solver, offline, on this machine.

One last poke. (ration the energy below a Strike's draw) Cut the generator fuel
below what a Strike needs, and Mete refuses to fake an answer. It says cannot
build tonight — recommend resupply, and tells you exactly how short you are. It
will not show you a green light it can't back up.

--- IF THEY ASK ABOUT THE MATH / "HOW DOES IT ACTUALLY WORK?" ---

The simple version (say this first):
  It's an optimizer. We give it three things: what each drone needs to do the
  job — range, payload, how much wind it can handle; what each drone costs to
  print — grams of filament, printer-hours, watts; and what we actually have on
  hand tonight. It finds the plan that covers the most mission value for the
  least material, without ever breaking a hard limit.

The honesty rule that makes it credible (say this next if they're nodding):
  Two rules keep it honest. One — a drone can only cover a target if it clears
  every requirement. That's a yes/no gate, not a score, so it never picks the
  big expensive drone just because it looks better; the big one only wins when
  it's the only one that can reach. Two — when two plans tie, we break the tie
  the exact same way every time, so the same inputs always give the same answer.

If they push for the real machinery:
  Under the hood it's an integer program — every decision is a 1 or a 0, "build
  this drone for this target, or don't" — and we solve it with Google's CP-SAT,
  the same solver class used for real logistics scheduling. We convert every
  number to whole integers so there's no floating-point fuzz, and we run it
  single-threaded with a fixed seed. That's why it's reproducible to the byte.
  With three drone types and a handful of targets it solves in well under a
  millisecond, so it can't choke on a number a judge throws at it.


--- "WHY NOT JUST USE AI / AN LLM FOR THIS?" ---
  We're not anti-AI — AI is great for reading the situation, like turning a
  sentence into a mission profile. But you should never let it make the
  decision. An LLM guesses, and it can't promise you the same answer twice. At
  the edge, a wrong number doesn't waste filament — it loses the mission. So we
  use math you can check, that gives the same answer every time, instead of a
  model you have to hope is right.


--- "DID YOU FAKE THE DEMO?" ---
  No — you just typed a number we'd never seen and it solved. The wifi's off, so
  there's no server doing it behind the scenes. It's a real solver running on
  this laptop. Same input, same answer, every time — we even threw a thousand
  weird inputs at it to make sure it never flinches.


--- "ARE THOSE NUMBERS REAL?" ---
  They're cited — payload to the DoD Group-1 weight class, energy to real
  battery numbers — and every single one is editable. We're not claiming we know
  your exact catalog; we're giving you a starting point you can correct. Change
  any number and watch it re-solve. It's a frontier, not an oracle.


--- "DOESN'T A SLICER ALREADY DO THIS?" ---
  A slicer plans how to print one part you already picked — the toolpath. We
  decide what to build and how many, across the whole fleet, when all your
  budgets are fighting each other. That five-to-one flip is a call a slicer
  never makes.


--- "IS THIS A COMPANY, OR JUST A FEATURE?" ---
  The optimizer is the wedge — it's how we get in the door. The company is what
  it becomes: the secure layer that decides and verifies what every node builds
  across the whole network. Plus the mission-value data piles up over time and
  gets more valuable. That's infrastructure, not a feature.


--- "WHAT STOPS A PRIME — OR FIRESTORM — FROM JUST BUILDING THIS?" ---
  Three things. It's deterministic and offline, which is hard to get right and
  we already did — we found and killed the one bug that breaks it. The model is
  cited and editable, so each customer tunes it to their own catalog and that
  data locks them in. And the real moat is the MOSA piece: becoming the trusted,
  tamper-proof gate every config file passes through before it touches a printer.


--- "WHO'S THE CUSTOMER? WHO PAYS?" ---
  Day one, it ships as the software brain inside expeditionary manufacturing
  kits — the people already moving printers to the edge, like FireStorm's xCell.
  We're the decision layer that makes that hardware actually useful at 2 a.m.
  when no one can call home.


--- "WHAT'S REAL TODAY VS. THE BIG MOSA VISION?" ---
  Everything we just showed is real and running offline right now — the
  optimizer, the live re-solve you drove, the resupply refusal, and Repair mode.
  The secure MOSA config hub is the roadmap — that's where the funding goes.
  We're being straight about that line on purpose.


--- "DOES IT SCALE?" ---
  Today it's tiny and instant on purpose — three drone types, a handful of
  targets, sub-millisecond. The exact same math scales to more types, more
  targets, and more nodes; this class of solver runs far bigger problems in
  industry every day. We kept the demo small so it's provably correct, not
  because it can't grow.


--- "WHAT IF SOMEONE FEEDS IT GARBAGE / A BAD FILE?" ---
  It rejects a malformed request at the door before the solver ever sees it. And
  the weird-but-valid stuff — a zero print speed, a negative cost — fails
  cleanly with a clear reason instead of crashing or lying. We tested over a
  thousand of those.
